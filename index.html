Case Description – ROSE the Care Robot

ROSE, developed by Heemskerk Innovative Technology, is a care robot used in elderly care homes. In our scenario, ROSE acts as both a nursing assistant and nutritionist—monitoring vital signs, tracking food intake, reminding medication, and offering companionship. By automating routine tasks, ROSE helps reduce caregiver workload and ensures consistent and personalized care.

Design Tools Used

  • Scenario-Based Tool: Mind Map → Storyboard → Scratch prototype
  • Expression-Based Tool: Uses color and sound to express emotion and intent, aiding inclusive communication
  • Embodiment-Based Tool: Card game for building robot appearance through iterative user-centered choices
  • Behavior-Based Tool: Behavior Tree with Context Swap to simulate unexpected events and test response design
  • Ethics-Based Tool: Mini Ethics Canvas worksheet for teams to assess social and ethical implications

Functional Breakdown

  • Monitor vitals (e.g., heart rate, hydration)
  • Track meal intake and dietary needs
  • Remind residents of medication schedules
  • Engage in basic conversation and emotional support
  • Report anomalies to staff in real-time

Potential Experimental Approach

We propose testing ROSE through simulation in a mock elderly care scenario using Scratch and behavior trees. Variables such as interruption handling, emotional engagement, and nutrition tracking accuracy will be evaluated. We also plan to use surveys to assess the perceived usefulness and acceptance among test participants.

Reflections

How is Design Research Linked to HRI?

HRI relies on iterative, user-centered design—scenario mapping, physical embodiment, and emotional expression are all rooted in design research approaches.

Digital AI vs. Embodiment

Embodiment adds physical presence, which creates trust, realism, and emotional bonding, unlike disembodied voice assistants.

Why is 'HER' (Not) a Compelling Argument?

‘HER’ explores emotional AI relationships, but lacks the tactile, contextual, and ethical complexity found in real-world HRI.

Reflection: The Robot Revolution – When?

The robot revolution is already happening in quiet sectors—elder care, warehousing, personal assistants. Mass acceptance hinges on trust and usability.

Reflection: The Dinosaur Hotel

The Henn-na Hotel in Japan replaced most of its robots with humans. It exposed the limitations of overly rigid automation in hospitality, where adaptability and empathy matter.

Reflection: Nabaztag

This iconic Wi-Fi rabbit pioneered ambient interaction. Despite its simplicity, it inspired deeper research into minimal affective communication in robotics.

Reflection: CuddleBits

Minimal robots like CuddleBits succeed emotionally despite lacking complex features. It highlights how "less" can feel more relatable.

Reflection: Be-right-back...

Social robots can fill temporary emotional gaps or practical roles, but shouldn't replace deep human presence—design must respect that balance.
